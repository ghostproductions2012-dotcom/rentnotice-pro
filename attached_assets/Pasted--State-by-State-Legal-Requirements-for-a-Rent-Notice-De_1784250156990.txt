# State-by-State Legal Requirements for a Rent-Notice Desktop App

## Executive summary

The most important engineering conclusion is that a single national “pay-or-quit” workflow is legally unsafe. States vary sharply on four high-risk dimensions: the pre-filing notice period, how days are counted, what service methods are valid, and whether a nonpayment notice may demand only base rent or may also include other charges. California, Alaska, and Massachusetts are especially clear that a rent-demand notice must not be inflated with non-rent charges; Florida is explicit about how partial rent affects a nonpayment notice; Maryland now requires a pre-filing notice-of-intent form for failure-to-pay-rent actions; Hawaii added a nonpayment mediation prerequisite effective February 5, 2026; and Oregon’s online ORS publication itself warns that the 2025 codification does not yet incorporate 2026 session changes, so any Oregon automation must ingest current-session laws rather than rely on the static ORS edition alone. citeturn17view2turn16view2turn14search0turn15view2turn26search8turn8search14turn38search7

For app design, the safest default nationwide is a **rent-only engine** with state overrides. The engine should begin with periodic base rent, subtract verified payments and credits, and exclude late fees, utility charges, repair charges, legal fees, deposits, parking, pet charges, and other ancillary items unless a state-specific attorney-reviewed rule expressly authorizes inclusion. That conservative design matches the most compliance-sensitive states and reduces dismissal risk in states whose statutes speak in terms of “rent” rather than broader “amount due.” citeturn17view2turn16view1turn14search0turn15view2

A production-ready desktop app should therefore ship with a **rule engine plus a source registry**, not just merge fields. Each state record should store: notice types; trigger conditions; day-count logic; allowed service methods; proof-of-service artifact requirements; mandatory language; filing prerequisites; holiday source; local exception registry; and a release status flag such as `draft`, `attorney_review_required`, or `approved`. For high-complexity jurisdictions such as California, New York, New Jersey, Connecticut, Washington, Maryland, Massachusetts, Oregon, and Hawaii, the initial release should be blocked until state templates and local overlays have had attorney review. The official and quasi-official sources reviewed here show why: local just-cause ordinances, mandatory informational forms, mediation programs, right-to-counsel overlays, and court form requirements frequently alter otherwise familiar eviction workflows. citeturn17view2turn12search2turn29search0turn39search7turn26search8turn14search7turn12search5turn8search14

The desktop app should also distinguish three legal layers. First, **statewide substantive law** establishes notice periods, default service rules, cure rights, and termination grounds. Second, **statewide judicial procedures and forms** control the affidavit, complaint, summons, and execution stages. Third, **local overlays** may add just-cause, relocation, filing declarations, language access rules, court diversion, or government notices. Los Angeles City and Los Angeles County add notable eviction-control layers; New York City’s housing-court ecosystem is far more demanding than a bare RPAPL summary; Maryland has Baltimore City-specific post-judgment property rules; Kansas City runs a local Right to Counsel program; and Connecticut has a statewide eviction Right to Counsel system that should be represented as a statewide workflow flag. citeturn13search12turn21search1turn12search17turn12search21turn26search12turn28search13turn39search7

What follows is organized for ingestion into template-generation software. Where a point could not be confidently confirmed from the sources reviewed in this session, it is labeled **unspecified** and paired with the best source bundle to verify before release.

## Methodology and implementation architecture

This report prioritizes primary and official sources where they were readily accessible in-session: state legislative code pages, state court self-help pages, attorney general handbooks, judiciary form packets, and official court instructions. Where an official source was unavailable or blocked, high-quality legal-aid or state-sponsored explanatory materials were used as secondary confirmation. In a few states, only a strong source bundle rather than an exact provision was confirmed during this session; those states are marked accordingly and should remain behind an “attorney review required” flag before any public rollout. citeturn15view2turn17view1turn17view2turn25search1turn30search0turn37search0turn29search0turn26search8

From an engineering standpoint, the cleanest architecture is a four-layer model. The **ledger layer** produces a rent-only arrears table by month. The **notice rule layer** determines what notice exists, the minimum period, the day-count method, and the content requirements. The **service layer** determines who may serve, how service may be proved, when mailing/posting is allowed, and what affidavit text or checkbox flow is required. The **local overlay layer** injects city or county addenda, including just-cause statements, relocation notices, mediation notices, language access forms, or right-to-counsel materials. California’s eviction self-help materials, for example, explicitly warn that many cities or counties require more detail than the statewide minimum, which is exactly the kind of local override the app should model as `required_addenda[]`. citeturn17view2turn17view1turn13search12turn21search7

The core validation philosophy should be conservative. A notice should be **blocked** whenever the notice amount includes anything other than rent in a state where the source reviewed clearly limits the demand to rent; when the app cannot determine that the service method selected is allowed; when a mandatory pre-filing form or information sheet is missing; when a mandatory cure-right statement is absent; or when the nonpayment deadline has been miscounted because weekends, business days, or court holidays were handled incorrectly. Other issues can be **warnings** rather than hard stops, such as ambiguous payment allocation, possible local just-cause overlay, possible federal CARES Act overlay, or uncertain lease-driven notice variation. citeturn17view2turn16view2turn15view2turn14search7turn37search0turn33search13

The app should treat holidays as a dynamic data source, not static hard-coded text. California’s court self-help page moves deadlines that fall on a weekend or court holiday to the next court day, and Florida expressly excludes Saturday, Sunday, and court-observed holidays from its 3-day nonpayment count. Utah’s standard 3-day nonpayment form uses **three business days**. Those examples are enough to justify one shared date engine with state-specific parameters, plus annual holiday sync from each state judiciary or court-administration holiday page. citeturn17view1turn15view2turn33search13

### What the matrix means for software

The table shows why the app should not store “notice period” as a single integer. You need at least these parameters: `days_count`, `day_type` (`calendar`, `business`, `court`), `exclude_weekends`, `exclude_state_holidays`, `exclude_court_holidays`, `mail_extension_days`, `requires_cure`, `requires_pre_filing_form`, `requires_notice_of_intent`, `requires_mediation`, `service_modes_allowed[]`, and `local_overlays_required[]`. That model is directly supported by California’s court-day counting and declaration language, Florida’s weekend-and-court-holiday exclusion plus email rule, Maryland’s notice-of-intent form and agreed e-service, Utah’s business-day form, and Alaska’s mail-extension/service-proof structure. citeturn17view1turn15view2turn26search17turn33search13turn16view2

## State implementation notes

The table above is the quickest ingestion format. For actual release planning, the states fall into four engineering cohorts.

### Rent-only strict states

California is the clearest example. Its 3-day notice to pay rent or quit must state the exact rent owed, how and where payment can be made, and it **cannot ask for late fees, utilities, or damages**. Its delivery proof should include the notice name, the delivery date, the delivery method, a California penalty-of-perjury statement, and the server’s signature. Alaska’s approved handbook likewise says the nonpayment form must state rent only and expressly says the amount does not include late fees; it also gives concrete proof-of-service options and a 3-day mail extension when registered or certified mail is used. Massachusetts likewise treats a Notice to Quit for nonpayment as a rent notice, not a fee notice; MassLegalHelp is explicit that a nonpayment notice cannot demand late fees, attorney’s fees, or constable fees, and Massachusetts now requires a form to accompany a residential notice to quit for nonpayment. These three states should be modeled as **hard blocks** against fee-inflated notices. citeturn17view2turn17view1turn16view2turn14search0turn14search7

For this cohort, your UI should display a month-by-month rent ledger, a separate excluded-charges panel, and a visible “Rent-only notice amount” subtotal. The system should force a user acknowledgment if any excluded category exists. It should also refuse to finalize if the calculated notice amount differs from the rent-only subtotal. That is the safest behavior for California, Alaska, and Massachusetts, and it is a defensible national default elsewhere. citeturn17view2turn16view1turn14search0

### States with unusual pre-filing prerequisites

Maryland is no longer a simple “file when rent is due” state. Before a landlord can file a failure-to-pay-rent complaint, Maryland sources now require written notice of intent and use of form DC‑CV‑115, giving tenants 10 days to pay. The notice may be mailed, taped to the door, or, if the tenant agreed, delivered by email, text, or a tenant portal. Maine requires the residential Notice to Quit to be served with an **Eviction Information Sheet and Mediation Request** form CV‑256. Hawaii, starting February 5, 2026, requires a 10-day written notice and, if the tenant requests it, participation in pre-filing mediation before a residential nonpayment-of-rent case may be filed. Those states should be modeled as `pre_filing_prerequisites[]` rather than ordinary notice states. citeturn26search8turn26search4turn26search17turn37search0turn37search8turn8search14turn8search20

Your app should therefore have a state-aware checklist engine. Before generating a filing packet, it should ask: “Was the required pre-filing form served?”, “Was mediation requested or completed if required?”, “Was the required notice-of-intent sent?”, and “Was the judicial information sheet attached?” If the answer is no in Maryland, Maine, or Hawaii, the app should block filing-packet generation outright. citeturn26search8turn37search0turn8search14

### Lease-sensitive or locality-sensitive states

Georgia, North Carolina, New Jersey, Missouri, Tennessee, Oregon, Wisconsin, and Minnesota should be handled as **attorney-review states** in a first commercial release unless your team completes a deeper state-pack review and local testing. New Jersey is the standout example: New Jersey courts and LSNJ are clear that, except for most nonpayment cases, the notice regime depends on the specific eviction ground and may require a notice to cease followed by a notice to quit. North Carolina is also tricky because Legal Aid publishes a common 10-day nonpayment notice framework while the North Carolina Judicial Branch says landlords are not always required to send an eviction notice. Oregon deserves a special engineer warning because the Oregon Legislature’s own ORS portal says the 2025 online edition does not yet incorporate all changes made in the 2025 special session and 2026 regular session. Wisconsin’s notice structure also varies with the tenancy and violation pattern. citeturn29search0turn29search20turn37search6turn37search14turn38search7turn34search1turn34search9

In those states, the app should not “guess” a single definitive statewide notice. Instead, the UI should require the user to choose from a rule card such as “lease-controlled nonpayment,” “month-to-month nonrenewal,” “curable lease violation,” or “statutory just-cause ground,” and then surface the supporting citation and template version. If the operator cannot identify the correct card, the software should save a draft but block finalization. citeturn29search0turn37search14turn38search7

### Local-overlay states

California, New York, Connecticut, Maryland, Washington, and Missouri all require a local-exception registry even if the statewide core rule is understood. California courts themselves warn that many cities or counties require more details. Los Angeles City requires certain no-fault filings and declarations through LAHD; Los Angeles County publishes tenant-facing eviction guidance with 3-, 30-, 60-, and 90-day notice structures. Connecticut’s statewide eviction Right to Counsel program means operator screens should surface counsel/referral materials where required by deployment design. Maryland has Baltimore City-specific eviction execution/property-removal rules on the judiciary housing page. Kansas City, Missouri has a local Right to Counsel program that is not statewide but is significant enough to justify a municipal workflow entry. citeturn17view2turn13search12turn21search1turn39search7turn26search12turn28search13

A local registry design should support `jurisdiction_key`, `effective_date`, `requires_registration`, `requires_declaration`, `requires_relocation_text`, `requires_counsel_notice`, `rent_control`, `just_cause_only`, and `additional_templates[]`. It should also support an annual review date because these local rules change faster than statewide code books. citeturn13search12turn21search1turn28search13turn39search7

## Suggested UI rules, blocking logic, and test cases

The app should separate **blocking rules** from **warning rules**.

Blocking rules should include: non-rent amounts in a rent-demand notice where the state pack is rent-only; missing tenant names, property address, or payment instructions; missing service details required for proof; generation of a nonpayment notice in Maryland without DC‑CV‑115 content; generation of a Maine residential notice without the information sheet; generation of a Hawaii nonpayment workflow without the mediation branch; generation of a California notice with an incorrectly counted 3-day deadline; and generation in Oregon from a stale 2025-only rule pack that ignores 2026 updates. citeturn17view2turn17view1turn26search8turn37search0turn8search14turn38search7

Warning rules should include: ambiguous payment allocation across months; lease-driven states where the selected state card may not match the lease form; possible federal overlay such as CARES Act or subsidized housing; possible local overlay such as Los Angeles, New York City, Baltimore City, Kansas City, or Connecticut RTC; and use of electronic service in a state where the reviewed source only authorizes it when the tenant agreed. citeturn15view2turn26search17turn13search12turn26search12turn28search13turn39search7

## Final deployment guidance

A rent-notice desktop app can be safely scaffolded statewide, but it should not present itself as a “50-state definitive legal engine” on day one. The sources reviewed here show that a defensible release path is: first, ship a strong rent-only ledger and notice-drafting core; second, ship state packs with verification tiers; third, require attorney review for complex states and local overlays; fourth, sync judicial holidays and session-law changes annually; and fifth, log every operator choice and version every template. That approach is especially important in states where the official materials themselves emphasize form precision, service precision, or rapidly changing law. citeturn17view1turn14search7turn26search8turn37search0turn38search7
